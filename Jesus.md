# The Biblical Jesus: God's Human Messiah, Agent, and Embodied Wisdom

## Introduction: The Question Beneath the Debate

Few questions have shaped Christian theology more deeply than this: Who is Jesus?

The traditional answer of much of historic Christianity is that Jesus is the God-man: the eternal Son, the second person of a triune Godhead, fully God and fully man. This answer was formally defined through later creeds and councils, especially in the fourth and fifth centuries, using terms such as essence, substance, hypostasis, eternal generation, and two natures.

The alternative explored here is Biblical Unitarian Christology. It affirms that there is one God, the Father, and that Jesus is God's uniquely begotten human Son, the promised Messiah, the sinless and obedient servant, the one whom God sent, empowered, raised, exalted, and appointed as Lord. Jesus is not reduced to an ordinary prophet or moral teacher. He is God's supreme agent, the embodied Wisdom and Word of God, the image of the invisible God, the mediator of salvation, the appointed judge, and the Lord of the new creation.

The central claim is simple: the New Testament's exalted language about Jesus is best read within the Jewish monotheistic world of Jesus and the apostles, not through later metaphysical categories. In that world, divine authority can be represented without multiplying God's identity. A sent agent can speak for the sender without being the sender. God's Wisdom and Word can become visible in a human life without requiring a second eternal person within God.

This view does not ask readers to honor Jesus less. It asks readers to honor him as Scripture presents him: not as God Himself, but as the Son of God, the Messiah whom the one God has made Lord.

## The One God Is the Father

The starting point of biblical faith is the confession of one God. Israel's Shema declares, "Hear, O Israel: the LORD our God, the LORD is one" (Deuteronomy 6:4). Jesus did not revise that confession into a tri-personal definition of God. When asked about the greatest commandment, he affirmed the same monotheistic confession (Mark 12:29).

The New Testament repeatedly identifies the one God as the Father while distinguishing Jesus as the one sent by Him. Jesus prays to the Father as "the only true God" and identifies himself as the Messiah whom that God sent (John 17:3). Paul writes, "For us there is one God, the Father... and one Lord, Jesus Christ" (1 Corinthians 8:6). He also says there is "one God" and "one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus" (1 Timothy 2:5). Ephesians speaks of "one God and Father of all" (Ephesians 4:6).

This pattern matters. If the apostles meant to redefine the one God of Israel as Father, Son, and Spirit together, it is striking that their ordinary language so often identifies the one God specifically as the Father. Jesus is normally called Son, Lord, Christ, servant, mediator, man, image, and heir. These are exalted titles, but they distinguish him from the God who sends, anoints, raises, and exalts him.

The Biblical Unitarian reading begins where Jesus begins: with the Father as the only true God. Every claim about Jesus must be interpreted within that confession, not over it.

## Jesus as God's Human Messiah

The earliest apostolic preaching presents Jesus as a man appointed and vindicated by God. Peter's sermon at Pentecost does not announce that God became man. It announces "Jesus of Nazareth, a man attested to you by God with mighty works and wonders and signs that God did through him" (Acts 2:22). Peter then proclaims that God raised Jesus and made him "both Lord and Christ" (Acts 2:36).

This pattern continues throughout Acts. God raised Jesus. God exalted Jesus. God anointed Jesus with the Holy Spirit and power. God appointed Jesus as judge of the world. The resurrection is not merely the return of a divine being to a prior heavenly state. It is God's public vindication of the faithful human Messiah.

Jesus' greatness is therefore not self-originating. It is God-given. His authority is real, universal, and saving, but it is derived from the Father. He receives authority, speaks words given by the Father, does works the Father empowers, and reigns at God's right hand. His lordship is not a rival lordship beside God; it is the lordship God grants to His obedient Son.

This preserves the full force of Jesus' humanity. Jesus grows in wisdom. He prays. He is tempted. He obeys. He suffers. He dies. He says, "Not my will, but yours, be done" (Luke 22:42). He calls the Father "my God" even after the resurrection (John 20:17). Hebrews presents him as a true human brother, a tested and sympathetic high priest, and one who learned obedience through suffering.

If Jesus is ontologically God, these features require later two-natures explanations. Biblical Unitarianism reads them more directly: Jesus is the faithful human Son, the last Adam, the obedient Messiah, and the true image-bearer through whom God restores humanity.

## Agency: The Sent One Represents the Sender

One of the strongest interpretive keys is the Jewish principle of agency, often summarized as: the agent is as the one who sent him. A commissioned agent could speak and act with the authority of the sender. The agent's words and actions counted as the sender's words and actions, while the agent remained personally distinct from the sender.

Scripture is full of this pattern. Moses is made "as God" to Pharaoh, not because he becomes divine, but because he bears God's authority. Israel's judges can be called "gods" because they administer divine justice. The angel of the LORD can speak in God's name and even in the first person as God, while remaining God's messenger. Kings, priests, prophets, and angels can bear divine authority representationally.

Jesus is the supreme fulfillment of this pattern. John's Gospel repeatedly describes him as sent by the Father. He says he can do nothing from himself, that his teaching is not his own, and that the words people hear from him are the Father's words. To receive Jesus is to receive the one who sent him. To see Jesus is to see the Father, because the Father is at work in and through him.

This explains how Jesus can speak God's words, reveal God's character, forgive sins, judge, receive honor, and bear divine titles without being ontologically identical with God. He is not a detached messenger with partial authority. He is God's perfect agent, the one in whom God's will is fully represented.

Agency preserves both sides of the biblical pattern: Jesus truly reveals God, and Jesus remains distinct from God.

## Wisdom and Word Made Flesh

Another major theme is Wisdom Christology. Jewish Wisdom literature often personifies God's Wisdom as present with God, active in creation, dwelling among people, and calling humanity to life. Proverbs 8, Sirach 24, and Wisdom of Solomon use vivid, elevated language for Wisdom. But this language functions as poetic personification of God's wisdom, plan, and self-expression. It does not require Wisdom to be a second divine person alongside God.

John's Logos language belongs in this world. "In the beginning was the Word" can be read as a statement about God's creative speech, reason, wisdom, and redemptive plan. The Word was with God because God's plan and self-expression belong to Him. The Word was God in the sense that it bore God's own character and quality. The Word became flesh when God's eternal purpose took historical form in the man Jesus.

This is not a low view of Jesus. It is a high Wisdom Christology. Jesus is the embodiment of God's Wisdom. He is the human life in which God's Word, purpose, character, and saving intention become visible. Paul can call Christ "the wisdom of God" because in him God's wise redemptive plan is enacted.

This approach also explains why New Testament writers can use cosmic and exalted language about Jesus while still distinguishing him from God. Jesus is the image of God, not the invisible God Himself. He is the radiance of God's glory, not the one whose glory he reflects. He is the appointed heir, not the God who appoints him. He is the firstborn, the preeminent one, the head of the new creation.

Wisdom and Word language allows the New Testament to say something enormous: God's own purpose has become flesh in Jesus. It does not require the later claim that a second divine person became human.

## Preexistence as Foreordination and Divine Plan

Some passages speak as though Jesus existed "before" his earthly life. Biblical Unitarianism does not ignore these texts. It asks what kind of preexistence is being described.

Jewish thought could speak of things as existing in God's plan before they appear in history. A prophet can be known before birth. Believers can be chosen before the foundation of the world. God can call things that are not as though they are. The Messiah can be foreknown, promised, and prepared before he is manifested.

In this framework, Jesus' preexistence can be ideal, prophetic, or purposeful rather than literal and personal. He exists beforehand in God's wisdom, decree, promise, and redemptive plan. The glory prepared for him can be spoken of as already present with God because God's purposes are certain.

This is especially important for texts in John. Language of coming from heaven can refer to divine mission and origin, not necessarily to a conscious heavenly life before birth. Jesus is "from above" because he is sent by God and speaks God's words. He is before Abraham because he is central to God's ancient promise and messianic purpose.

The question is not whether Jesus is eternally important. He is. The question is whether biblical language of foreknowledge, mission, and Wisdom requires belief in a pre-human divine person. The Biblical Unitarian answer is no.

## Reading the Major Christological Texts

Several passages are often used as decisive proof that Jesus is God. Read within Jewish monotheism, agency, Wisdom, and exaltation, they can be understood differently.

### John 1:1-14

Trinitarian theology often reads the Word as a preexistent divine person, God the Son, who becomes human. The Wisdom-agency reading sees the Word as God's self-expression, plan, and creative speech. The text speaks of "Word" before it speaks of "Son." The Word becomes flesh when God's purpose is embodied in Jesus, who reveals the unseen Father.

### John 8:58

"Before Abraham was, I am" is often connected to God's self-identification in Exodus. But the Greek phrase "I am" can function as ordinary or emphatic self-identification. In John's context, Jesus is revealing himself as the Messiah appointed in God's plan before Abraham, not necessarily claiming to be the God of Israel.

### John 10:30

"I and the Father are one" is often read as ontological unity. But the context concerns the security of the sheep in the hands of Jesus and the Father. Jesus immediately appeals to Psalm 82, where divine language is applied to human representatives. John 17 also uses unity language for believers. The natural reading is unity of mission, will, and purpose.

### John 20:28

Thomas' confession, "My Lord and my God," is a climactic recognition of the risen Jesus. Yet the same chapter has Jesus say, "I ascend to my Father and your Father, to my God and your God" (John 20:17). The Gospel has already identified the Father as the only true God. Within agency, Thomas' words can honor the God revealed in and through the risen Messiah without overturning the Father-Son distinction.

### Philippians 2:5-11

This passage is often read as a story of a divine person descending into humanity. But it can also be read as the story of the obedient Messiah. Unlike Adam, who grasped at being like God, Jesus does not grasp. He humbles himself, obeys unto death, and therefore God highly exalts him. The logic of the passage is not that Jesus returns to a status he already possessed, but that God exalts him because of his faithful obedience.

### Colossians 1:15-20

Christ is the image of the invisible God and the firstborn over creation. "Image" means representation, not identity. "Firstborn" often denotes rank, inheritance, and supremacy. The passage names thrones, dominions, rulers, and authorities, and then identifies Jesus as head of the body and firstborn from the dead. This can be read as new-creation Christology: the risen Messiah is supreme over the reconciled order God brings about through him.

### Hebrews 1

Hebrews presents the Son as God's final revelation, appointed heir, radiance of glory, exact representation, and enthroned royal Son. These are very high claims. Yet they are framed by appointment, inheritance, representation, and enthronement. The Son reflects God, inherits from God, and is anointed by God. The passage exalts Jesus without collapsing him into the God he represents.

### Titus 2:13 and 2 Peter 1:1

These texts are sometimes cited as direct identifications of Jesus as God, but their Greek syntax is debated. Even if one allows a high reading, ambiguous passages should not overturn the clearer and repeated New Testament pattern: the Father is the one God, and Jesus is His sent Messiah.

## High Christology Without Ontological Deity

Biblical Unitarianism does not deny high Christology. It contests what high Christology means.

Jesus is Lord, Son of God, image of God, Wisdom of God, mediator, judge, Savior, firstborn, heir, and exalted ruler. These are immense claims. But high status does not automatically mean identity with the one God.

The key distinction is between functional supremacy and ontological identity.

Functional supremacy means Jesus acts with God's authority, reveals God's will, governs God's new creation, receives honor, and mediates salvation because God has appointed and exalted him. Ontological identity means Jesus is the same God as the Father in being or essence.

The Biblical Unitarian claim is that the first category is deeply biblical, while the second is a later theological synthesis. The New Testament can affirm the highest possible role for Jesus under God without making Jesus the one God Himself.

## Worship, Honor, and Glory

One of the strongest Trinitarian objections concerns worship. If Jesus receives worship, does that not prove he is God?

The answer depends on how worship and honor function in biblical context. The Greek word often translated "worship" can include homage, reverence, bowing, or royal honor, depending on context. Kings and representatives can receive homage without being God. The decisive question is whether Jesus receives honor as the one true God or as the one whom God has exalted.

The New Testament's pattern is mediated devotion. Jesus is honored as Lord to the glory of God the Father. Honoring the Son honors the Father who sent him. The Lamb is distinguished from the One seated on the throne while receiving exalted honor. At the end, the Son is subject to God, so that God may be all in all (1 Corinthians 15:28).

This does not make devotion to Jesus optional or shallow. Jesus is the risen Lord. He is owed allegiance, obedience, reverence, and confession. But the final direction of glory remains the Father, the one God who sent and exalted him.

## The Holy Spirit as God's Power and Presence

Biblical Unitarian theology also tends to read the Holy Spirit as God's active presence, power, breath, and gift rather than as a third co-equal person. The Spirit is poured out, given, received, and distributed. Jesus is anointed with the Spirit. Believers are empowered by the Spirit. God's Spirit is God Himself at work among His people.

This preserves the biblical reality of the Spirit without requiring a later Trinitarian definition. God acts by His Spirit through His Messiah and in His people.

## The Historical Development of Trinitarian Doctrine

The doctrine of the Trinity did not appear fully formed in the preaching of Jesus or the apostles. It developed over time as the church engaged Greek philosophical vocabulary, debated Christ's status, and responded to controversy.

The Council of Nicaea in 325 used the term homoousios, "same substance," to define the Son's relation to the Father. The Council of Constantinople in 381 consolidated Trinitarian doctrine, including the Spirit. Later Christological formulations further defined Jesus in terms of two natures.

This history does not require the conclusion that later theologians were insincere or careless. Many were trying to protect Jesus' exalted status and the coherence of salvation. But it does mean that later creedal grammar should be treated as interpretation, not as the starting point for reading first-century Jewish texts.

The apostolic confession is simpler: one God, the Father; one Lord Messiah, Jesus; one Spirit of God at work. The later doctrinal categories may be historically important, but they should not be imposed backward as the obvious meaning of the biblical text.

## The Trinitarian Case for Divine Identity

A fair discussion should acknowledge the best Trinitarian case. It is not merely that Jesus has divine-sounding titles. Jewish agency can explain that. It is not merely that Wisdom language is exalted. Jewish personification can explain that.

The central argument is that earliest Christian devotion gives Jesus a unique place in worship, invocation, creation language, sovereignty, judgment, and scriptural identification. Some scholars argue that this pattern includes Jesus within the unique divine identity of Israel's God.

This is a serious argument. But even here, the move from early Jesus-devotion to Nicene ontology is a theological inference. The New Testament does not explicitly say that God is three co-equal, co-eternal persons, or that Jesus is one hypostasis sharing one ousia with the Father. Those are later conceptual tools used to interpret the data.

The issue is therefore not whether Jesus occupies a uniquely exalted place. He does. The issue is whether first-century Jewish evidence requires that exalted status to be read as ontological deity rather than as supreme delegated agency.

## A Biblical Unitarian Reply

The reply begins by granting the weight of the evidence. Biblical Unitarianism must not reduce Jesus to one agent among many. He is the uniquely begotten Son, the definitive revealer of God, the Lord through whom salvation, judgment, and new creation are administered.

Yet the New Testament's highest claims about Jesus are still repeatedly framed by distinction, reception, and mediation: God sends him, gives him authority, raises him, exalts him, appoints him, and receives glory through him. Even texts about worship, judgment, creation, glory, and divine naming must be read within that governing pattern rather than abstracted from it.

John 1, preexistence language, Thomas' confession, and the worship of the Lamb are therefore not dismissed, but interpreted through Jewish categories of Word, Wisdom, agency, and exaltation. These texts show that God's own self-expression and saving purpose are uniquely present in Jesus. They do not by themselves require the later conclusion that Jesus is a second co-equal person within the being of God.

The Trinitarian reading is possible as a theological synthesis, but it is not the only way to honor the full biblical witness. Biblical Unitarianism argues that Scripture gives enough conceptual resources to confess Jesus as God's supreme human Messiah and exalted Lord without redefining the one God as Father, Son, and Spirit.

## The Gospel: God Through His Messiah

The gospel does not require incarnation metaphysics to be powerful. It proclaims that the one God of Israel fulfilled His promises by sending Jesus, His Son and Messiah. Jesus proclaimed the Kingdom, obeyed the Father perfectly, healed by God's power, taught God's words, suffered faithfully, died for sins, and was raised from the dead. God exalted him to His right hand, made him Lord and Christ, appointed him judge, and grants forgiveness and life through him.

This gospel preserves the reality of Jesus' obedience. His temptation is real. His suffering is real. His death is real. His resurrection is God's decisive vindication. His lordship is God's gift. His mediation matters because he is truly human and truly appointed.

The saving story is not "God became man so God could save." It is "God saves through the man He anointed, raised, and exalted." Salvation is from the Father, through the Son, by the Spirit.

## Implications for Faith and Practice

This Christology reshapes Christian faith in several important ways.

First, it restores the Jewish roots of Christian confession. Jesus and the apostles worshiped the one God of Israel. A Father-centered monotheism keeps Christian faith anchored in that world.

Second, it makes Jesus' example directly meaningful. Jesus is not a God pretending to live a human life. He is the faithful human Son who shows what Spirit-filled obedience looks like.

Third, it clarifies worship. The Father is the one true God. Jesus is honored as the Lord Messiah through whom we come to the Father. The Spirit is God's living power and presence among His people.

Fourth, it simplifies proclamation. The apostolic message is not first a metaphysical formula about divine essence. It is the announcement that God has raised Jesus and made him Lord and Christ.

Fifth, it opens clearer dialogue with Jews and Muslims, who share a concern for strict monotheism. The claim that Jesus is God's Messiah remains distinctive and challenging, but it does not require redefining God as a tri-personal being.

## Conclusion: The Son of God, Not God the Son

The cumulative case is this: the New Testament identifies the one God as the Father; presents Jesus as God's human Messiah; describes Jesus as sent, empowered, raised, exalted, and appointed by God; uses agency to explain divine representation; uses Wisdom and Word language to describe God's purpose embodied in Jesus; and proclaims a gospel centered on God acting through His Son.

Jesus is not merely a prophet, teacher, or moral example. He is God's supreme agent, Messiah, Son, Lord, Wisdom-bearer, image, mediator, last Adam, and appointed judge. He reveals God perfectly because God acts through him perfectly. He is worthy of honor because the Father has given him authority. He is central to God's redemptive plan because in him God's Word, Wisdom, Kingdom, and new creation reach their appointed fulfillment.

The highest Christology faithful to the one God of Israel is not "God the Son," but "the Son of God."

There is one God, the Father, the only true God. There is one Lord Messiah, Jesus, the man whom God sent, empowered, raised, exalted, and appointed over all things. Through him, God grants forgiveness, life, judgment, and new creation. To believe in Jesus is to believe in the one whom God has sent. To honor Jesus is to glorify the Father who made him Lord.

That is the biblical Jesus: God's human Messiah, God's faithful agent, God's Wisdom embodied, and God's exalted Lord.
