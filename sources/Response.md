# What Interpretive Framework Should Guide New Testament Christology?

Yes: **historically responsible exegesis should begin with the linguistic and conceptual world of Second Temple Judaism**, not with fourth-century metaphysical vocabulary. Later Trinitarian doctrine may be a theological synthesis of the New Testament, but it should not be smuggled in as the starting premise.

That said, the Jewish-contextual framework should not be reduced to **agency and Wisdom** alone. Those are necessary categories, but not sufficient categories unless they can account for the full pattern of earliest Christian belief and practice.

A sound framework would ask:

**1. What can Jewish agency explain?**
Quite a lot. The Jewish law of agency allowed a sent representative to act with the authority of the sender; the classic rabbinic formula is that “a man’s agent is like himself.” ([jewishencyclopedia.com][1]) So sayings about Jesus speaking God’s words, executing God’s judgment, revealing God, bearing God’s name, or being honored as God’s authorized envoy do not automatically prove ontological deity. They may be agency language.

**2. What can Wisdom/Word traditions explain?**
Also quite a lot. Sirach 24 personifies Wisdom as coming from God, dwelling among Israel, and being identified with Torah; the USCCB note explicitly describes Wisdom as “personified as coming from God, yet distinct from him,” and says this background is reflected in Johannine Logos language. ([USCCB][2]) So preexistent “Word/Wisdom” language need not automatically mean a second divine person in later Nicene terms.

**3. Where does agency/Wisdom begin to strain?**
The key issue is not whether Jesus can act for God. Jewish agents can do that. The sharper question is whether Jesus is included in patterns that Second Temple Jews normally reserved for the one God: worship/invocation, the divine name, creation of all things, universal sovereignty, and eschatological judgment.

This is why scholars such as Larry Hurtado and Richard Bauckham have argued that the earliest Christian evidence is not merely “high titles” but a **devotional and identity pattern**. Hurtado describes earliest Christian devotion as directed to “the one God of biblical tradition, and to the one ‘Lord’ Jesus,” while still defining Jesus with reference to God and as God’s agent/expression. ([Larry Hurtado's Blog][3]) Bauckham’s proposed first-century Jewish criterion is that Israel’s God was uniquely identified as the sole Creator, sole sovereign Ruler, and rightful recipient of worship. ([ntslibrary.com][4]) ([ntslibrary.com][4])

That does not settle the debate, but it gives a historically Jewish criterion: **not “does Jesus have divine-sounding titles?” but “is Jesus included in what marks out the unique identity and worship of Israel’s God?”**

For example, 1 Corinthians 8:6 distinguishes “one God, the Father” and “one Lord, Jesus Christ,” yet also places creation and existence “through” Christ. ([Bible Hub][5]) Revelation 5 depicts the Lamb receiving honor, glory, and praise in the heavenly throne scene. ([Bible Gateway][6]) And the early Christian practice of “calling on the name of the Lord Jesus Christ” is significant because “calling on the name of the Lord” was a worship-associated phrase rooted in Israel’s Scriptures; one study notes that the phrase is not used of pre-Christian Jewish mediators but is applied in the New Testament to Jesus. ([The Gospel Coalition][7])

So the strongest Trinitarian historical argument is **not**: “the New Testament explicitly says Nicene metaphysics.” It does not. Nor is it: “Jesus is called God, therefore Trinity.” That is too quick.

The stronger argument is: **within a Jewish monotheistic setting that allowed agents and personifications, the earliest Christians nevertheless gave Jesus a unique place in worship, invocation, creation, sovereignty, and divine-scriptural identification that exceeds ordinary agency.**

But your challenge remains valid: no New Testament text says, in later creedal language, “Jesus is a co-equal, co-eternal divine person within a triune Godhead.” That formulation is a later theological grammar. The exegetical question is whether that later grammar faithfully accounts for the New Testament’s pattern better than an agency/Wisdom-only model.

My answer would be:

**The proper interpretive framework is Second Temple Jewish monotheism, including agency, Wisdom, exalted mediator traditions, and Jewish worship boundaries.** Within that framework, agency explains much of the data, but the cumulative pattern of earliest Jesus-devotion is the main historical reason many scholars think the New Testament goes beyond mere agency. Still, the move from that pattern to Nicene ontology is a theological inference, not a self-evident lexical fact.

So later metaphysical conclusions should not be treated as the default reading. They should be treated as **post-biblical doctrinal interpretations whose legitimacy depends on whether they best explain the first-century Jewish evidence**, not as the starting point for exegesis.
