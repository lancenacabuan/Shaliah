# Validation for Choosing Biblical Unitarianism over Trinitarian Tradition

This document compiles the strongest distinct reasons, from the existing project files and representative online sources, for preferring Biblical Unitarianism over later Trinitarian tradition. Here "valid points" means arguments that are exegetically, historically, or conceptually substantive. Some are decisive only when taken cumulatively, but each contributes to the case that the New Testament can be read more naturally through Jewish monotheism, agency, and Wisdom/Word categories than through later Nicene metaphysics.

## Core Thesis

Biblical Unitarianism is preferable when the interpretive starting point is the first-century Jewish and apostolic context: the one God is the Father, Jesus is God's uniquely begotten and exalted human Messiah, and the high language applied to Jesus is best explained through divine agency, Wisdom/Word embodiment, resurrection exaltation, and delegated authority rather than ontological identity with God.

The Trinitarian tradition may be a later theological synthesis, but the Biblical Unitarian claim is that it should not be treated as the default meaning of the biblical texts.

## 1. The New Testament Identifies the One God as the Father

- John 17:3 is programmatic: Jesus calls the Father "the only true God" and distinguishes himself as the one whom the Father sent.
- 1 Corinthians 8:6 distinguishes "one God, the Father" from "one Lord, Jesus Christ." Biblical Unitarians argue that Paul does not identify the one God as Father, Son, and Spirit, but specifically as the Father.
- 1 Timothy 2:5 distinguishes "one God" from "one mediator between God and mankind, the man Christ Jesus." This remains true after Jesus' resurrection and exaltation.
- Ephesians 4:6 speaks of "one God and Father of all," preserving the Father-centered language of Jewish monotheism.
- The ordinary New Testament use of "God" overwhelmingly refers to the Father, while Jesus is normally called Messiah, Son, Lord, servant, mediator, or man.

Validation: If the apostles meant to redefine Israel's one God as a tri-personal being, the repeated identification of the one God as the Father alone is unexpectedly direct and persistent.

## 2. Jesus Is Presented as God's Human Messiah, Not as God Himself

- Peter's Pentecost sermon calls Jesus "a man attested to you by God" through miracles that "God did through him" (Acts 2:22). This is apostolic proclamation at the birth of the church.
- Acts 2:36 says God made Jesus "Lord and Christ," implying appointment and exaltation by God.
- Jesus prays to God, obeys God, is sent by God, is raised by God, is exalted by God, receives authority from God, and remains subject to God.
- Jesus says "the Father is greater than I" (John 14:28) and "my God and your God" after the resurrection (John 20:17).
- Hebrews emphasizes Jesus' genuine humanity, testing, obedience, priesthood, and mediation.

Validation: Biblical Unitarianism gives full force to the New Testament's recurring pattern: God acts through Jesus, raises Jesus, and appoints Jesus, rather than Jesus being the same God who does those things.

## 3. Jewish Agency Explains High Representative Language

- The Jewish shaliah principle states that an authorized agent can speak and act on behalf of the sender so fully that the agent's words and actions are legally attributed to the sender.
- Scripture contains precedents where agents or representatives bear divine language without being God by nature: Moses is "as God" to Pharaoh (Exodus 7:1), judges can be called "gods" in Psalm 82, and the angel of the LORD can speak in God's name.
- John's Gospel is saturated with sending language: Jesus does not speak from himself, does not act from himself, and repeatedly grounds his authority in the Father who sent him.
- John 10:30, "I and the Father are one," can be read as unity of mission and purpose, especially in light of John 17 where Jesus prays that believers may be one as he and the Father are one.
- John 10:34-36 uses Psalm 82 to defend "Son of God" language on the basis of consecration and sending, not a direct claim to ontological deity.

Validation: Agency explains why Jesus can reveal God, speak God's words, receive honor, judge, forgive, and act with divine authority while remaining distinct from the one who sent him.

## 4. Wisdom and Word Language Need Not Mean a Second Divine Person

- Jewish Wisdom literature personifies Wisdom in vivid terms without requiring Wisdom to be a separate person within God.
- Proverbs 8, Sirach 24, Wisdom of Solomon 7, and related traditions provide a Jewish grammar for God's wisdom, word, plan, and self-expression.
- John 1 can be read as God's Word/Wisdom becoming embodied in the man Jesus, not as an eternal divine person becoming human in later conciliar categories.
- The Logos can be understood as God's creative and revelatory self-expression, plan, or reason.
- Jesus is called the wisdom of God because he embodies and enacts God's wise redemptive purpose.

Validation: Biblical Unitarianism can affirm high Wisdom/Logos Christology while avoiding the later metaphysical claim that the Logos is a co-equal divine person.

## 5. Pre-Existence Language Can Be Read as Foreordination or Ideal Pre-Existence

- Jewish thought could speak of realities as existing in God's plan before they appear in history.
- The Messiah, Torah, repentance, or names can be described as prepared or foreknown before the ages without implying literal personal existence.
- Texts about Jesus' glory, mission "from heaven," or being "before" others may be read through divine foreknowledge, predestination, and mission language.
- 1 Peter 1:20 says Christ was foreknown before the foundation of the world and manifested in the last times, a pattern compatible with preordination.

Validation: Biblical Unitarianism can account for pre-existence language through Jewish categories of foreknowledge and divine plan without requiring a literal pre-human divine person.

## 6. Major Trinitarian Proof Texts Are Interpretively Disputed

- John 1:1 depends partly on the grammar of an anarthrous theos and the meaning of Logos in Jewish context.
- John 8:58 can be read as messianic self-identification rather than a direct citation of the divine name.
- John 20:28 is a climactic confession, but it occurs in a Gospel where Jesus also says the Father is his God and the only true God.
- Philippians 2:6-11 can be read as a Second Adam or obedient-Messiah hymn: Jesus does not grasp at status, humbles himself, and is therefore exalted by God.
- Colossians 1:15-20 uses image, firstborn, creation, and reconciliation language that can be read through Wisdom, rank, inheritance, and new creation.
- Hebrews 1 uses royal, Wisdom, and representative language; the Son is the radiance and exact representation of God, which implies representation rather than identity.
- Titus 2:13 and 2 Peter 1:1 involve debated Greek syntax and should not bear the weight of defining God as triune.

Validation: If the central proof texts require complex interpretive argument, they should not override clearer, repeated statements identifying the Father as the one God and Jesus as God's sent Messiah.

## 7. Exaltation Christology Fits the Apostolic Gospel

- The apostolic proclamation in Acts centers on God raising and exalting Jesus.
- Jesus becomes Lord and Christ by God's appointment, not by being recognized as the same God who appointed him.
- Psalm 110:1 distinguishes YHWH from "my lord," with the Messiah seated at God's right hand by divine grant.
- Resurrection vindicates Jesus as Messiah, Son of God, judge, and Lord.
- Jesus' lordship is derivative and God-given, exercised "to the glory of God the Father" (Philippians 2:11).

Validation: Biblical Unitarianism preserves the gospel pattern of God accomplishing salvation through His obedient Messiah and then exalting him.

## 8. Jesus' Genuine Humanity Is Easier to Preserve

- Jesus grows in wisdom, learns obedience, is tempted, suffers, dies, and depends on God.
- A truly human mediator is central to 1 Timothy 2:5 and Hebrews.
- If Jesus is ontologically God, his growth, learning, temptation, ignorance, dependence, and death require later two-natures explanations that are not explicit in the New Testament.
- Biblical Unitarianism sees Jesus' obedience as genuinely human obedience empowered by God, not the activity of an omniscient divine person behind a human nature.

Validation: Biblical Unitarianism gives a simpler account of Jesus as the faithful second Adam, human Messiah, and model of obedience.

## 9. The Holy Spirit Is Often Described as God's Power and Presence

- Biblical Unitarians commonly read the Holy Spirit as God's active presence, power, breath, and gift rather than a distinct co-equal person.
- The Spirit is poured out, given, received, and distributed.
- The Spirit's work is God's own action in believers and in Jesus' ministry.
- Trinitarian personhood language for the Spirit is much more developed in later doctrine than in explicit biblical definition.

Validation: This preserves strict monotheism while still affirming the real activity of God's Spirit.

## 10. Trinitarian Doctrine Uses Later Technical Categories

- The New Testament does not use the terms Trinity, one substance, homoousios, hypostasis in the later doctrinal sense, eternal generation, or two natures.
- The doctrine was formalized through post-apostolic controversy, especially at Nicaea and Constantinople, and then further refined in later Christological councils.
- Biblical Unitarianism argues that later creedal grammar should be evaluated as interpretation, not assumed as the original apostolic framework.
- Early Christian history shows diversity in Christological models before later orthodoxy hardened into official doctrine.

Validation: If a doctrine requires later metaphysical vocabulary to state clearly, it should not be treated as the obvious meaning of first-century Jewish texts.

## 11. The Simplicity of Jewish Monotheism Is Preserved

- The Shema declares one God, and Jesus affirms Israel's monotheistic confession.
- Biblical Unitarianism maintains that God is one personal being, the Father.
- It avoids the logical and linguistic tension of saying three distinct persons are each God while there is only one God.
- It aligns more naturally with Jewish monotheistic categories familiar to Jesus, Paul, and the earliest disciples.

Validation: Biblical Unitarianism preserves the simplest reading of biblical monotheism without requiring a redefinition of "one God" into one essence shared by three persons.

## 12. The Father Receives Ultimate Worship as God, While Jesus Receives God-Given Honor

- Jesus directs worship to the Father as the true worshipers' object of worship (John 4:23-24).
- Jesus teaches prayer to the Father and prays to the Father himself.
- Philippians 2 says every tongue confesses Jesus as Lord "to the glory of God the Father."
- Honoring Jesus can be understood as honoring the Father who sent and exalted him.
- Biblical Unitarianism can affirm reverence, obedience, and allegiance to Jesus as Lord without making him the one God.

Validation: This keeps the biblical distinction between ultimate source and appointed mediator: worship terminates in God, while Jesus is honored as God's supreme agent and exalted Lord.

## 13. The Doctrine of the Trinity Is Not Explicitly Stated

- No biblical passage says God is three co-equal, co-eternal persons.
- No apostolic sermon in Acts defines God as Father, Son, and Holy Spirit.
- Triadic formulas such as Matthew 28:19 or 2 Corinthians 13:14 mention Father, Son, and Spirit together, but do not define them as one triune God.
- The absence of explicit trinitarian definition matters because the doctrine is later treated as central and essential.

Validation: Biblical Unitarianism argues from what Scripture repeatedly says plainly, rather than from a later synthesis of scattered texts.

## 14. Biblical Unitarianism Avoids Overloading Ambiguous Divine Titles

- "God," "god," "lord," "son of God," and "image of God" can function differently depending on context.
- Human judges, kings, angels, and agents can receive elevated titles representationally.
- "Lord" is not always equivalent to YHWH; it can identify master, ruler, Messiah, or exalted authority.
- "Son of God" in Jewish and biblical usage often denotes Messiahship, kingship, divine appointment, or miraculous origin, not ontological deity.

Validation: Biblical Unitarianism resists making every divine-sounding title a metaphysical claim.

## 15. The Gospel Remains Coherent without Incarnation Metaphysics

- God initiates salvation.
- Jesus obeys as the faithful human Messiah.
- God works through Jesus in miracles, teaching, death, resurrection, exaltation, and judgment.
- Jesus mediates between God and humanity because he is human and appointed by God.
- Believers come to the Father through the one whom the Father sent.

Validation: The saving story does not require God to become man; it requires God to act through the man He anointed, raised, and exalted.

## 16. Practical and Theological Advantages

- It restores the Jewish roots of Christian confession.
- It offers a simpler creed: one God, the Father; one Lord Messiah, Jesus; one Spirit of God at work.
- It makes Jesus' example more directly imitable as a genuinely human life of obedience.
- It reduces conceptual barriers in dialogue with Jews and Muslims who object to tri-personal formulations of God.
- It distinguishes biblical authority from later ecclesiastical authority.

Validation: These practical advantages do not prove the doctrine by themselves, but they reinforce the coherence of the Biblical Unitarian reading.

## Condensed Case

Biblical Unitarianism is strongest when its points are taken together:

1. The Father is repeatedly called the one God.
2. Jesus is repeatedly distinguished from God.
3. Jesus is explicitly called a man and mediator.
4. God sends, empowers, raises, exalts, and appoints Jesus.
5. Jewish agency explains divine representative authority.
6. Wisdom/Word traditions explain high Christological language without requiring a second divine person.
7. Major Trinitarian proof texts are disputed and context-dependent.
8. The Trinity was formalized with later technical vocabulary.
9. The apostolic gospel centers on God acting through His Messiah.
10. The Unitarian model preserves simple Jewish monotheism while still giving Jesus supreme Messianic honor.
