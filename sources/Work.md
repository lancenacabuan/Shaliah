# **God-Man or God’s Man?**

## **Jesus, Divine Agency, and Wisdom Christology in Second Temple Jewish Monotheism**

### **A Critical Comparison of Church-Traditional Trinitarianism and the Biblical Unitarian View of Jesus Christ**

---

## Abstract

This study examines whether the New Testament presents Jesus Christ as **ontologically God**, as later Nicene and post-Nicene Trinitarian theology maintains, or as **God’s uniquely authorized human Messiah**, as argued by Biblical Unitarian readings of early Christology. The project approaches this question through Second Temple Jewish monotheism, Jewish Wisdom traditions, messianic expectation, and the Hebrew-Jewish principle of agency.

The central argument is that much New Testament Christological language can be read coherently within Jewish categories of **divine representation, royal sonship, Wisdom embodiment, prophetic commission, and eschatological agency**. Jesus speaks God’s words, performs God’s works, judges on God’s behalf, reveals God’s character, and receives exalted honor because he is God’s appointed Messiah and supreme agent. This does not necessarily require identifying him as a second divine person within the being of God.

Classical Trinitarian theology later interpreted these texts through the language of divine essence, hypostasis, eternal generation, and consubstantiality. The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy summarizes the broad Trinitarian claim as the confession that the one God exists as or in three equally divine persons, with Father, Son, and Spirit understood as consubstantial. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][1]) The Council of Nicaea became decisive in this development by using *homoousios*, “of one substance,” to confess the Son’s relation to the Father. ([Encyclopedia Britannica][2])

This study does not deny that the New Testament presents Jesus in astonishingly exalted terms. Rather, it asks whether those terms require Nicene ontology or whether they are better explained by the Jewish grammar of **one God, one Messiah, one supreme agent, one eschatological Lord**.

---

# Central Thesis

The New Testament presents Jesus as **God’s uniquely begotten, anointed, exalted, and authorized human Messiah**, not as “God the Son” in the later Nicene sense. Jesus is the definitive agent of the one God of Israel: the one through whom God reveals Himself, inaugurates new creation, grants life, executes judgment, forgives sins, rules the Kingdom, and reconciles all things.

The New Testament’s highest Christological language is best understood through the convergence of four Jewish categories:

| Category        | Function in Christology                                                   |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Agency**      | Jesus acts, speaks, judges, and rules on God’s behalf.                    |
| **Wisdom**      | Jesus embodies God’s creative, revelatory, and redemptive purpose.        |
| **Messiahship** | Jesus is the royal Son of David, appointed to rule God’s Kingdom.         |
| **Exaltation**  | God raises Jesus and grants him lordship, authority, and universal honor. |

The completed argument may be stated this way:

> Jesus is not merely a prophet, teacher, or moral example. He is God’s climactic human representative: the Messiah, Son of God, Lord, Wisdom-bearer, image of God, last Adam, and ruler of the coming Kingdom. Yet the one God whom Jesus reveals, obeys, worships, prays to, and serves remains the Father.

---

# Methodology

This study uses a **historical-exegetical method** before moving to theological synthesis. It does not begin with Nicene categories as the controlling framework. Nor does it assume in advance that Biblical Unitarianism must be correct. Instead, it asks which model best explains the textual evidence in its Jewish historical context.

The study evaluates four interpretive models:

| Model                           | Core Claim                                                                                           |
| ------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Nicene Trinitarianism**       | Jesus is eternally God the Son, consubstantial with the Father.                                      |
| **Divine Identity Christology** | Jesus is included within the unique divine identity of Israel’s God.                                 |
| **Wisdom Christology**          | Jesus is described through language associated with God’s Wisdom, Word, image, and creative purpose. |
| **Agency Christology**          | Jesus is God’s authorized representative, who bears divine authority without being God Himself.      |

The agency model receives particular attention because Jewish legal tradition defines the *sheluaḥ* or *shaliaḥ* as an authorized representative whose actions legally bind the sender; the Jewish Encyclopedia summarizes the principle as “a man’s agent is like himself.” ([Jewish Encyclopedia][3])

# Part I — Historical and Conceptual Foundations

## Chapter 1 — Introduction: The Central Christological Question

The central question of Christian theology is not simply whether Jesus is important, exalted, heavenly, or saving. All major Christian traditions affirm that he is. The sharper question is this:

**Does the New Testament identify Jesus as the one God of Israel, or as the one God’s supreme human agent?**

Traditional Trinitarianism answers that Jesus is the eternal Son, personally distinct from the Father but sharing the same divine essence. Biblical Unitarianism answers that Jesus is the human Messiah, miraculously begotten, sinless, exalted, and uniquely authorized by God, but not ontologically God.

This project argues that the New Testament’s basic pattern is consistently relational and representative: God sends Jesus; Jesus obeys God; God empowers Jesus; God raises Jesus; God exalts Jesus; God grants Jesus authority; Jesus ultimately subjects the Kingdom to God. That pattern is difficult to reconcile with the claim that Jesus is the same God as the Father in an ontological sense, but it fits naturally within Jewish messianic agency.

## Chapter 2 — The Historical Development of Trinitarian Dogma

The doctrine of the Trinity did not appear fully formulated in the New Testament. It developed through centuries of theological debate over Scripture, worship, salvation, Greek metaphysics, anti-heretical polemic, and ecclesial identity.

The decisive fourth-century question concerned the relation of the Son to the Father. Was the Son created, subordinate, similar in essence, or of the same essence? The Council of Nicaea answered by confessing the Son as *homoousios* with the Father. Britannica summarizes Nicaea as the council that rejected Arianism and used *homoousios* to enshrine Christ’s divinity in a formal statement of faith. ([Encyclopedia Britannica][2])

This chapter argues that Nicene theology is a later doctrinal synthesis. It may be judged by Christians as a legitimate development, but historically it should not be assumed to be identical with the conceptual world of the New Testament itself.

## Chapter 3 — Jewish Monotheism and the Second Temple Context

Second Temple Jewish monotheism was not a bare mathematical claim that only one heavenly being existed. Jewish literature could speak of angels, heavenly beings, personified attributes, exalted patriarchs, royal figures, and divine agents. Yet Jewish monotheism remained centered on the unique supremacy of the God of Israel.

James McGrath argues that early Christian monotheism fits within a first-century Jewish context focused on one supreme God as uniquely worthy of worship. ([University of Illinois Press][4]) This is important because it challenges two oversimplifications: first, that high Christology must automatically mean Trinitarian ontology; second, that Jewish monotheism had no conceptual space for exalted agents.

The New Testament emerges within this Jewish symbolic world. Its Christology must therefore be interpreted first through Jewish categories before being translated into later creedal metaphysics.

## Chapter 4 — The Hebrew Concept of Divine Agency

Agency is central to this study. In Jewish law, an agent represents the sender. The agent does not become the sender ontologically, but the agent may act with the sender’s authority. The Jewish Encyclopedia defines agency as the legal relation in which a *sheluaḥ* acts by direction of the principal and can bind the principal in relation to a third party. ([Jewish Encyclopedia][3])

This provides a powerful lens for reading the New Testament. Jesus repeatedly says that he was sent, that his teaching is not his own, that his authority is given, that his works are the Father’s works, and that whoever receives him receives the one who sent him.

Agency explains how Jesus can reveal God without being God, speak for God without being identical to God, judge for God without being the ultimate source of judgment, and receive honor as God’s representative without displacing God.

## Chapter 5 — Agency Representation in the Hebrew Scriptures

The Hebrew Scriptures frequently present God as acting through representatives. Moses speaks as God’s mouthpiece. Angels bear the divine name. Prophets speak in the first person for YHWH. Kings rule as God’s anointed. Priests mediate divine forgiveness. The Davidic king can be called God’s son without being God Himself.

This chapter establishes a pattern: biblical representation can be extraordinarily strong. A figure may bear God’s authority, words, titles, and functions without being ontologically identical to God.

That pattern prepares the reader to understand Jesus as the supreme case of divine representation.

# Part II — Wisdom Traditions and Messianic Expectations

## Chapter 6 — Wisdom Traditions in Second Temple Judaism

Jewish Wisdom literature gives language to God’s creative and revelatory activity. Wisdom is described as present with God, active in creation, radiant with divine glory, and involved in the instruction of the righteous.

Wisdom of Solomon 7:25–26 describes Wisdom as a breath of God’s power, an emanation of divine glory, a reflection of eternal light, and an image of divine goodness. ([Bible Gateway][5]) Such language is strikingly elevated, yet it need not imply that Wisdom is a second divine person alongside God. It may function as personified divine attribute, poetic hypostatization, or theological symbol.

This chapter argues that Wisdom traditions gave early Christians a vocabulary for describing Jesus as the embodiment of God’s creative and redemptive plan.

## Chapter 7 — Wisdom Christology and the Origins of New Testament Christological Language

New Testament descriptions of Jesus as image, firstborn, radiance, Word, and agent of creation are often read as proof of personal preexistence. But Wisdom Christology allows another possibility: Jesus embodies God’s preexistent purpose without personally existing as a divine being before his birth.

James D. G. Dunn’s work is relevant here because he argued that early Logos/Wisdom Christology need not originally have been conceived in personally preexistent terms. ([Sage Journals][6]) This supports the possibility that elevated language about Christ may refer to God’s eternal plan now realized in the Messiah.

The key distinction is between:

**personal preexistence** — Jesus consciously existed before birth;
and
**ideal or prophetic preexistence** — Jesus existed beforehand in God’s plan, promise, Wisdom, and foreknowledge.

## Chapter 8 — The Son of God in Jewish Messianic Expectation

“Son of God” is often misunderstood as an automatic claim to deity. In the Hebrew Bible and Jewish messianic expectation, however, sonship is frequently royal, covenantal, representative, and vocational.

Israel is God’s son. The Davidic king is God’s son. The Messiah is God’s son. This title indicates chosen status, covenant intimacy, royal authority, and divine appointment.

Therefore, the confession that Jesus is the Son of God does not by itself require the conclusion that Jesus is God the Son. In a Jewish messianic frame, “Son of God” means the anointed royal representative of God.

## Chapter 9 — The Functional Use of Divine Titles in Biblical Literature

Biblical language often applies divine-sounding titles functionally. Human judges may be called “gods” because they exercise delegated judgment. Kings may be called sons of God. Angels may speak as God’s representatives. Prophets may utter divine speech in the first person.

This chapter argues that the application of divine titles to Jesus must be interpreted carefully. The question is not merely, “Is Jesus called God?” The deeper questions are:

What does the title mean in context?
Is it absolute or representative?
Is the Father still distinguished as Jesus’ God?
Is the title ontological, royal, functional, or honorific?

# Part III — New Testament Christology Reconsidered

## Chapter 10 — The New Testament Portrait of Jesus as God’s Authorized Agent

The New Testament repeatedly portrays Jesus as the one sent by God. His authority is derivative, not independent. He is the Messiah whom God anoints, empowers, raises, glorifies, and appoints.

This agency pattern is especially clear in John. Jesus does not act from himself. He speaks what he hears. He does the Father’s works. He seeks the Father’s glory. He is the sent one.

This does not diminish Jesus. It defines his greatness. His authority is supreme precisely because it is God-given.

## Chapter 11 — Reexamining the Johannine Prologue: John 1:1–18

John 1 is one of the most important Christological texts in the New Testament. Traditional interpretation reads the Logos as the personally preexistent Son who becomes incarnate. A Biblical Unitarian reading understands the Logos as God’s self-expression, Wisdom, plan, command, or revelatory purpose, which becomes embodied in the man Jesus.

On this reading, “the Word became flesh” does not mean that a second divine person transformed into a human. It means that God’s self-revealing Wisdom and purpose became historically embodied in Jesus the Messiah.

This interpretation coheres with the wider biblical pattern in which God’s word creates, reveals, commands, judges, and saves. Jesus is the final and fullest embodiment of that divine self-expression.

## Chapter 12 — Philippians 2:5–11 and the Adamic-Messianic Paradigm

Philippians 2 is often read as a descent-ascent narrative: the preexistent divine Son descends from heaven, becomes human, dies, and is exalted. But the text may also be read as an Adamic and messianic contrast.

Jesus, though in the “form of God,” does not grasp at equality. Instead, he humbles himself, obeys, suffers, and is exalted by God. The NRSVUE rendering emphasizes that Christ did not treat equality with God as something to be grasped, but humbled himself in obedience. ([Bible Gateway][7])

The Adamic reading argues that Jesus is the obedient human image of God. Adam grasped; Jesus submitted. Adam disobeyed; Jesus obeyed. Adam sought exaltation; Jesus received exaltation from God.

The climax confirms this: God highly exalts Jesus and grants him the name above every name. Jesus does not seize divine status; he receives authority from God.

## Chapter 13 — Colossians 1:15–20 and the Language of New Creation

Colossians 1 calls Jesus the image of the invisible God and the firstborn of all creation. It says that all things are created in, through, and for him. ([Bible Gateway][8]) The traditional reading understands this as a statement about Jesus’ role in Genesis creation. The agency-Wisdom reading asks whether the passage concerns the new creation centered on the risen Messiah.

The immediate context emphasizes reconciliation, redemption, the church, resurrection, and cosmic restoration. Jesus is the “firstborn” especially as the beginning of the new humanity. He is the image of God because he is the true human and messianic ruler through whom God restores creation.

This chapter does not deny the cosmic scope of Colossians 1. It argues that the cosmic scope is eschatological and redemptive rather than necessarily ontological.

## Chapter 14 — Hebrews 1 and the Royal Sonship Tradition

Hebrews 1 presents the Son as the definitive revelation of God, superior to angels, enthroned at God’s right hand, and addressed in royal language. It also uses imagery close to Jewish Wisdom traditions, especially in describing the Son as radiance and imprint.

The key question is whether Hebrews 1 identifies Jesus as God Himself or as the exalted royal Son through whom God now speaks. The chapter’s opening says that God has spoken “in a Son,” which already establishes distinction between God and the Son. The Son is heir, appointed, enthroned, and exalted.

Hebrews 1 is therefore a strong high-Christology text, but it can be interpreted as royal-Wisdom agency Christology rather than Nicene ontology.

## Chapter 15 — Thomas’s Confession: John 20:28

Thomas’s confession, “my Lord and my God,” is one of the strongest texts used for the deity of Christ. A Trinitarian reading takes it as direct address to Jesus as God. A Biblical Unitarian reading asks how this confession functions within John’s Gospel as a whole.

John’s Gospel repeatedly distinguishes the Father as the one who sends Jesus. John 17:3 identifies the Father as “the only true God” and Jesus as the one sent by Him. ([Bible Gateway][9]) Therefore, Thomas’s confession should not be isolated from John’s broader theology of agency and revelation.

Possible interpretations include direct confession, representative confession, or recognition that God is uniquely revealed in the risen Jesus. The strongest Unitarian reading is that Thomas sees God in and through Jesus, the Father’s supreme agent and revealer.

## Chapter 16 — The Meaning of *Theos* Applied to Jesus

The New Testament rarely applies *theos* directly to Jesus. When it may do so, the interpreter must distinguish between absolute deity and representative or royal usage.

Biblical language allows “god” terminology to function in more than one way. A being may be called “god” because of delegated authority, judicial function, divine representation, or exalted status. Therefore, merely finding the term *theos* applied to Jesus does not settle the question of ontology.

The controlling question is whether Jesus is ever identified as the same God whom he worships, obeys, prays to, and calls Father.

## Chapter 17 — The “I Am” Sayings and Their Literary-Historical Context

The Johannine “I am” sayings are often interpreted as direct allusions to the divine name. Some may indeed echo divine self-revelation language. But they also function literarily as messianic-revelatory declarations within John’s Gospel.

Jesus is the bread, light, door, shepherd, resurrection, way, truth, life, and vine. These sayings reveal who Jesus is as the one sent by God. Their purpose is not necessarily to identify Jesus as YHWH Himself, but to reveal Jesus as the definitive mediator of God’s life and truth.

In John, Jesus’ identity is inseparable from his mission. He is the one who reveals God because God sent him.

## Chapter 18 — Preexistence: Personal Ontology or Prophetic Foreordination?

Preexistence language is one of the most important debates in Christology. Traditional theology reads many passages as teaching Jesus’ literal personal preexistence. Biblical Unitarian interpretation distinguishes between literal preexistence and ideal preexistence.

In Jewish thought, realities can exist beforehand in God’s plan, promise, Wisdom, or decree before appearing historically. The Messiah may be foreknown, foreordained, and spoken of as existing in God’s purpose before his birth.

This chapter concludes that some New Testament texts can be read as personal preexistence, but they do not require it when read through Jewish Wisdom and foreordination categories.

# Part IV — Monotheism, Christology, and Theological Synthesis

## Chapter 19 — Jesus and the One God of Israel

The New Testament maintains a consistent distinction between God and Jesus. God sends; Jesus is sent. God raises; Jesus is raised. God exalts; Jesus is exalted. God gives authority; Jesus receives authority. God is the source; Jesus is the mediator.

This relational pattern strongly supports agency Christology. Jesus is not a rival to God, nor an independent divine being. He is the one through whom the one God acts.

The question, therefore, is not whether Jesus is exalted. The question is how his exaltation relates to the identity of the one God.

## Chapter 20 — Pauline Monotheism and the Christological Reformulation of the Shema

1 Corinthians 8:6 is central. Paul writes of “one God, the Father” and “one Lord, Jesus Christ.” ([Bible Gateway][10]) Richard Bauckham argues that this text includes Jesus within the divine identity of the God of Israel, and his work has significantly shaped modern discussion of “divine identity” Christology. ([Logos][11])

A Biblical Unitarian reading agrees that Paul gives Jesus an extraordinary role, but argues that Paul still identifies the “one God” as the Father. Jesus is the one Lord through whom God’s purposes are accomplished, but he is not numerically identical with the one God from whom all things come.

Thus, Paul’s language may be read as a messianic expansion of monotheistic confession without becoming a metaphysical redefinition of God’s being.

## Chapter 21 — The Father as the Sole True God in Early Christian Theology

Several New Testament texts explicitly identify the Father as the one God. John 17:3 is especially important because Jesus addresses the Father as the only true God and distinguishes himself as the one whom the Father sent. ([Bible Gateway][9])

This chapter argues that the Father’s identity as the one God is not peripheral but foundational. Jesus’ own God-centered language must be given full theological weight.

Biblical Unitarian Christology therefore begins with the confession:

**There is one God, the Father, and one mediator, the man Messiah Jesus.**

## Chapter 22 — Counterarguments from Classical Trinitarian Theology

The strongest Trinitarian arguments must be treated seriously. These include:

1. Jesus receives worship or cultic devotion.
2. Jesus is involved in creation.
3. Jesus forgives sins.
4. Jesus judges the world.
5. Jesus bears divine titles.
6. Jesus is associated with divine glory.
7. Jesus is invoked in prayer.
8. Jesus is placed within monotheistic confession.

Larry Hurtado’s work is especially important because he argued that devotion to Jesus emerged very early within Jewish Christian circles, not simply as a later Hellenistic development. ([The Gospel Coalition][12])

The Biblical Unitarian response is not to deny early devotion to Jesus, but to ask whether such devotion identifies Jesus as God or honors him as God’s exalted agent. The New Testament can direct honor to Jesus “to the glory of God the Father,” as Philippians 2 itself states. ([Biblia][13])

## Chapter 23 — Assessing Nicene and Post-Nicene Christological Formulations

Nicene theology sought to preserve the full saving significance of Christ. It affirmed that the Son is not a creature and not merely similar to the Father, but of the same essence. The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy notes that Trinitarian doctrine is commonly expressed through the claim that Father, Son, and Spirit are equally divine persons within the one God. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][1])

This chapter argues that Nicene theology may be coherent as later dogmatic theology, but it is not the most natural reading of the New Testament’s own categories. The New Testament more often speaks in terms of mission, obedience, exaltation, agency, sonship, lordship, and mediation than in terms of essence, nature, or eternal generation.

The issue is not whether Nicene theology can incorporate biblical language. It can. The issue is whether the biblical authors themselves required Nicene metaphysics to communicate their message.

## Chapter 24 — The Coherence of Wisdom Christology and Agency Christology

Wisdom Christology and agency Christology are mutually reinforcing.

Wisdom Christology explains why Jesus can be described in cosmic, revelatory, and image-of-God language. Agency Christology explains why Jesus remains subordinate, obedient, sent, empowered, exalted, and dependent on God.

Together they produce a coherent early Christology:

Jesus is the human Messiah in whom God’s Wisdom, Word, purpose, and saving power are embodied.

# Part V — Evaluation and Conclusions

## Chapter 25 — Comparative Analysis: Ontological Deity versus Representative Authority

| Question                  | Nicene Trinitarian View                 | Biblical Unitarian / Agency View                           |
| ------------------------- | --------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| Who is Jesus?             | Eternal God the Son incarnate           | God’s uniquely begotten human Messiah                      |
| Who is the one God?       | The triune God: Father, Son, and Spirit | The Father alone                                           |
| What is Jesus’ authority? | Intrinsic divine authority              | Delegated authority from God                               |
| What is Jesus’ lordship?  | Expression of divine identity           | Messianic exaltation granted by God                        |
| What is preexistence?     | Personal eternal preexistence           | God’s foreknown plan, Wisdom, or purpose embodied in Jesus |
| What is worship of Jesus? | Worship of God the Son                  | Honor given to God’s exalted agent to the Father’s glory   |
| What is incarnation?      | God the Son assumes human nature        | God’s Word/Wisdom becomes embodied in the human Messiah    |
| What is salvation?        | God personally saves in Christ          | God saves through His appointed Messiah                    |

## Chapter 26 — Evaluating the Historical and Exegetical Evidence

The Biblical Unitarian model has several strengths:

It preserves the New Testament’s distinction between God and Jesus.
It takes seriously the Father’s identification as the one God.
It explains Jesus’ dependence, obedience, prayers, temptations, death, exaltation, and mediation.
It situates Christology within Jewish monotheism rather than later metaphysics.
It accounts for divine titles through representation and agency.

The Trinitarian model also has strengths:

It accounts for the extraordinary height of New Testament Christological language.
It explains early devotion to Jesus.
It gives theological weight to texts that associate Jesus with creation, judgment, glory, and divine honor.
It provides a later systematic framework for Christian worship and salvation.

The decisive question is whether the Trinitarian model is exegetically necessary. This study concludes that it is not. The New Testament’s data can be coherently explained through Jewish messianic agency, Wisdom embodiment, and exaltation Christology.

## Chapter 27 — The Identity of Jesus within Biblical Monotheism

Jesus’ identity is best understood within biblical monotheism as follows:

He is not the God of Israel.
He is not a second God.
He is not merely an angel.
He is not merely a prophet.
He is not merely a moral teacher.
He is not simply an inspired human.

He is the Messiah: the uniquely begotten Son, the last Adam, the image of God, the Wisdom-bearer, the appointed Lord, the mediator of the new covenant, and the coming judge of the living and the dead.

James McGrath’s thesis that developed New Testament Christologies can still fit within first-century Jewish monotheism is important here because it shows that high Christology and Jewish monotheism are not mutually exclusive. ([Amazon][14])

## Chapter 28 — The Mission of the Messiah and the Restoration of God’s Kingdom

The New Testament is not primarily concerned with abstract ontology. It is concerned with God’s Kingdom.

Jesus announces the Kingdom.
Jesus embodies the Kingdom.
Jesus dies for the sins of the people.
God raises Jesus from the dead.
God exalts Jesus to His right hand.
Jesus reigns until all enemies are defeated.
The Kingdom is finally subjected to God.

The mission of Jesus is therefore the restoration of creation under the rule of the one God. Jesus is the agent through whom God accomplishes this restoration.

## Chapter 29 — Conclusion: God-Man or God’s Man?

The evidence suggests that the earliest Christology of the New Testament is better described as **messianic, Wisdom-shaped, and agency-based** than as fully Nicene.

Jesus is God’s man: not in the weak sense of being merely pious, but in the strongest biblical sense. He is the man whom God appointed, anointed, vindicated, raised, exalted, and enthroned. He is the man through whom God’s own rule, wisdom, word, judgment, mercy, and saving power are made known.

Thus, the final answer to the title question is:

**Jesus is not the God-man of later metaphysical dogma, but God’s man: the definitive human agent of the one God, the Father.**

---

# Final Condensed Argument

The New Testament presents Jesus in the highest categories available within Jewish monotheism. He is Son of God, Lord, Messiah, image of God, Wisdom-bearer, heavenly mediator, eschatological judge, and ruler of the Kingdom. Yet the same New Testament consistently distinguishes him from God, identifies the Father as the one God, portrays Jesus as sent and empowered, and describes his authority as received rather than intrinsic.

The later Nicene tradition interprets this exalted language ontologically: Jesus is God the Son, eternally consubstantial with the Father. The Biblical Unitarian interpretation reads the same evidence representatively: Jesus is God’s supreme agent, the one in whom God’s Wisdom and saving purpose are embodied.

The second reading better preserves the New Testament’s Jewish monotheistic grammar. Jesus does not replace God, rival God, or become a second divine center. He reveals God, obeys God, serves God, and reigns for God.

The Father remains the one God.
Jesus remains the one Lord Messiah.
The Kingdom remains God’s Kingdom.
The glory given to Jesus returns finally to God the Father.

# Final Thesis Statement

**The New Testament does not require the conclusion that Jesus is ontologically God. Rather, when read within Second Temple Jewish monotheism, Hebrew agency, Wisdom traditions, and messianic expectation, Jesus is best understood as God’s uniquely authorized human Messiah: the one through whom the Father speaks, acts, judges, saves, reconciles, rules, and restores creation. The later doctrine of the God-man is a post-biblical metaphysical development; the New Testament’s dominant portrait is that of God’s man, exalted to God’s right hand and appointed Lord of all.**
