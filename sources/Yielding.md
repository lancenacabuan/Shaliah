# Yielding the Evidence

The New Testament's high language about Jesus need not yield the conclusion that Jesus is ontologically God. When the texts are allowed to speak within their Jewish context, they yield a portrait of Jesus as God's uniquely authorized human Messiah: the Son, Lord, agent, Wisdom-bearer, image, mediator, and coming judge through whom the one God acts.

This reading does not lower Jesus. It locates his greatness where the apostolic witness locates it: in God's appointment, Jesus' obedience, resurrection vindication, exalted lordship, and representative authority.

## 1. Monotheism Yields the Father as the One God

The clearest monotheistic texts yield a Father-centered confession rather than a tri-personal definition of God.

Key yield:

- Deuteronomy 6:4 and Mark 12:29 preserve Israel's confession of one God.
- John 17:3 has Jesus address the Father as "the only true God."
- 1 Corinthians 8:6 distinguishes "one God, the Father" from "one Lord, Jesus Christ."
- 1 Timothy 2:5 distinguishes the one God from the one mediator, "the man Christ Jesus."
- Ephesians 4:6 speaks of "one God and Father of all."

Resulting argument:

The evidence yields the pattern "one God, the Father; one Lord Messiah, Jesus" rather than "one God in three co-equal persons." The latter is a later theological synthesis, not the plain grammar of the apostolic confession.

## 2. Apostolic Preaching Yields an Exalted Human Messiah

The sermons in Acts yield a proclamation centered on God's action through Jesus, not on Jesus as God Himself.

Key yield:

- Acts 2:22 presents Jesus as "a man attested" by God through works God did through him.
- Acts 2:36 says God made Jesus Lord and Christ.
- Acts 5:30-31 says God raised and exalted Jesus.
- Acts 10:38 says God anointed Jesus with Holy Spirit and power.
- Acts 17:31 says God will judge the world through a man He appointed.

Resulting argument:

The earliest public proclamation yields exaltation Christology: God vindicates and enthrones His Messiah. Jesus' lordship is real and supreme under God, but it is derived, granted, and exercised to God's glory.

## 3. Agency Yields Divine Authority Without Ontological Identity

The Jewish shaliah principle yields a way to understand how Jesus can speak and act for God without being the same being as God.

Key yield:

- Exodus 7:1 shows Moses functioning as "God" to Pharaoh by commission.
- Psalm 82 shows human representatives called "gods" because they bear divine judgment.
- The angel of YHWH can speak in God's name while remaining a messenger.
- John repeatedly describes Jesus as sent by the Father.
- John 13:20 states the agency logic directly: receiving the sent one means receiving the sender.

Resulting argument:

The evidence yields representative Christology. Jesus reveals God because God authorizes, indwells, empowers, and speaks through him. Agency explains divine titles, works, judgment, forgiveness, and honor without collapsing Jesus into the one who sent him.

## 4. Wisdom and Logos Yield Embodiment, Not Necessarily Personal Preexistence

Jewish Wisdom and Word traditions yield a strong framework for high Christology without requiring a second divine person.

Key yield:

- Proverbs 8 personifies Wisdom as present with God in creation.
- Sirach 24 and Wisdom of Solomon 7-10 describe Wisdom in elevated, poetic, and theological language.
- John 1 speaks of the Word becoming flesh in Jesus.
- 1 Corinthians 1:24 calls Christ the wisdom of God.
- Colossians 1 and Hebrews 1 use image, radiance, firstborn, and representative language.

Resulting argument:

The evidence yields Wisdom embodiment: God's plan, Word, Wisdom, and self-expression become historically visible in Jesus. The Logos "becoming flesh" need not mean a divine person changes into a human being; it can mean God's eternal purpose is realized in the human Messiah.

## 5. Preexistence Language Yields Foreordination and Divine Plan

Some preexistence language yields naturally to Jewish categories of foreknowledge, promise, and ideal existence.

Key yield:

- Jeremiah 1:5 shows a prophet known before birth.
- Romans 4:17 speaks of God calling things that are not as though they are.
- Ephesians 1:4 says believers are chosen before the foundation of the world.
- 1 Peter 1:20 says Christ was foreknown before the foundation of the world and manifested in the last times.
- John 17's glory language can be read as glory prepared in God's purpose.

Resulting argument:

The evidence yields ideal or prophetic preexistence rather than forcing literal personal preexistence in every case. Jesus exists beforehand in God's promise, Wisdom, decree, and redemptive plan before he appears in history.

## 6. John 1 Yields God's Word Embodied in Jesus

John 1 yields one of the strongest tests of the project's method.

Key yield:

- Genesis 1 and Psalm 33:6 present God's word as His creative command.
- Isaiah 55:10-11 presents God's word as going forth to accomplish His purpose.
- Proverbs 8 provides the Wisdom background.
- John 1:14 says the Word became flesh.
- John 1:18 says the Son reveals the unseen God.

Resulting argument:

John's Prologue yields a Wisdom/Word embodiment reading. The text says "Word" before it says "Son," allowing the Logos to be read as God's self-expression and purpose. That Word becomes flesh when God's revelatory purpose is embodied in Jesus.

## 7. John 10 Yields Unity of Mission

John 10 yields unity of action and purpose rather than a direct metaphysical definition.

Key yield:

- John 10:28-30 concerns the security of the sheep in the hands of Jesus and the Father.
- John 10:34-36 immediately appeals to Psalm 82 and to Jesus being sanctified and sent.
- John 17:21-23 uses similar unity language for believers.

Resulting argument:

"I and the Father are one" yields unity of mission, will, and protective power. The claim is high, but John's own context frames it through agency and shared purpose, not later essence-language.

## 8. John 20:28 Yields Climactic Representative Confession

Thomas' confession yields a powerful recognition of the risen Jesus, but it should be read inside John's whole theology.

Key yield:

- John 20:28 records Thomas saying, "My Lord and my God."
- John 20:17 has the risen Jesus refer to the Father as "my God and your God."
- John 17:3 has already identified the Father as the only true God.
- John 14:9-11 explains that seeing Jesus reveals the Father because the Father works in him.

Resulting argument:

John 20:28 yields a climactic confession of Jesus as the risen Lord who uniquely reveals God. In an agency framework, Thomas' words can honor the God revealed in and through Jesus without overturning John's repeated distinction between the Father and the sent Son.

## 9. John 8:58 Yields Messianic Self-Identification

The Johannine "I am" sayings yield strong revelatory claims, but not every use requires a direct assertion of ontological deity.

Key yield:

- John 8:58 uses *ego eimi*.
- John 9:9 uses the same phrase for ordinary self-identification by the healed blind man.
- John 4:25-26 uses similar language in a messianic disclosure.
- John 18:5-8 uses the phrase dramatically in the arrest scene.

Resulting argument:

John 8:58 yields messianic and revelatory self-identification: Jesus is the one appointed in God's plan before Abraham. The text is weighty, but its Greek wording and Johannine usage do not require it to be a direct citation of the divine name.

## 10. Philippians 2 Yields Obedient Adamic Messiah

Philippians 2 yields a pattern of humility, obedience, and God-given exaltation.

Key yield:

- Adam grasped at being like God; Jesus did not grasp.
- The suffering servant is humbled and then exalted.
- Philippians 2:8 says Jesus became obedient to death.
- Philippians 2:9 says "therefore God highly exalted him."
- Philippians 2:11 says the confession of Jesus as Lord is to the glory of God the Father.

Resulting argument:

The hymn yields an Adamic and messianic reading. Jesus, as the true image of God, refuses self-exaltation and yields himself fully to God's will. His exaltation is not self-possession of divine status but God's response to obedient sonship.

## 11. Colossians 1 Yields New-Creation Supremacy

Colossians 1 yields cosmic and redemptive supremacy centered on the risen Messiah.

Key yield:

- Jesus is the image of the invisible God.
- "Firstborn" can denote rank and inheritance.
- The passage names thrones, dominions, rulers, and authorities.
- Colossians 1:18 identifies Jesus as head of the body and firstborn from the dead.
- The passage climaxes in reconciliation.

Resulting argument:

Colossians 1 yields new-creation Christology. Jesus is supreme over the reconciled order God brings into being through him. The language is cosmic, but it can be read as eschatological and redemptive rather than as a direct statement that Jesus is the Genesis creator.

## 12. Hebrews 1 Yields Royal-Wisdom Sonship

Hebrews 1 yields very high Christology, but it does so through appointment, inheritance, representation, and enthronement.

Key yield:

- God speaks "in a Son."
- The Son is appointed heir.
- The Son is radiance and exact representation.
- Psalm 2 and Psalm 45 provide royal sonship background.
- Hebrews 1:9 says the Son has a God who anoints him.

Resulting argument:

Hebrews 1 yields the exalted Son as God's definitive representative. The Son reflects God, inherits from God, is enthroned by God, and is addressed in royal language. Representation remains distinguishable from identity.

## 13. Worship and Honor Yield Glory to the Father

New Testament devotion to Jesus yields real honor and allegiance, but the final direction of glory remains the Father.

Key yield:

- John 4:23-24 identifies the Father as the object of true worship.
- John 5:23 says honoring the Son honors the Father who sent him.
- Philippians 2:11 directs confession of Jesus as Lord to the glory of God the Father.
- Revelation 5 distinguishes the Lamb from the One seated on the throne while giving the Lamb exalted honor.
- 1 Corinthians 15:28 says the Son is finally subject to God.

Resulting argument:

The evidence yields mediated worship and representational honor. Jesus is honored as Lord because God exalted him. That honor does not compete with the Father; it magnifies the Father who sent, raised, and enthroned him.

## 14. Jesus' Humanity Yields Real Obedience

Jesus' yielding to God is one of the strongest internal patterns in the New Testament.

Key yield:

- Jesus grows in wisdom.
- Jesus prays to God.
- Jesus says the Father is greater than he is.
- Jesus says "not my will, but yours be done."
- Jesus learns obedience through suffering.
- Jesus dies and is raised by God.
- Jesus remains mediator as "the man Christ Jesus."

Resulting argument:

The evidence yields genuine humanity. Jesus' obedience is not a staged appearance behind which an omniscient divine person cannot really fail. It is the faithful human obedience of the last Adam, the Son who perfectly yields to the Father.

## 15. Later Doctrine Yields Development, Not Starting Point

Church history yields doctrinal development from apostolic proclamation to conciliar metaphysics.

Key yield:

- The New Testament does not use the terms Trinity, homoousios, hypostasis in the later doctrinal sense, eternal generation, or two natures.
- Nicaea 325 uses *homoousios* to define the Son's relation to the Father.
- Constantinople 381 consolidates pro-Nicene Trinitarian confession including the Spirit.
- Chalcedon 451 formalizes two-natures Christology.

Resulting argument:

The historical evidence yields development. Later doctrine may be meaningful as church theology, but it should not be treated as the neutral or obvious reading lens for first-century Jewish texts.

## 16. Trinitarian Proof Texts Yield Disputed Readings

The strongest Trinitarian proof texts yield serious interpretive debate rather than uncontested conclusions.

Key yield:

- John 1 depends on Logos background and grammar.
- John 8:58 depends on *ego eimi* usage and context.
- John 20:28 depends on how Thomas' confession relates to John's Father-Son distinction.
- Philippians 2 depends on *morphe*, *harpagmos*, and the hymn's narrative logic.
- Colossians 1 depends on "firstborn," "all things," and old-creation versus new-creation context.
- Hebrews 1 depends on royal Psalm usage and Wisdom imagery.
- Titus 2:13 and 2 Peter 1:1 depend on debated Greek syntax.

Resulting argument:

The proof texts yield complexity. Because they are context-dependent and disputed, they should not override the clearer recurring pattern: the Father is God, Jesus is His sent Messiah, and God acts through Jesus.

## 17. The Gospel Yields Simplicity Without Diminishing Jesus

The gospel yields a coherent saving story without requiring incarnation metaphysics.

Key yield:

- God initiates salvation.
- God sends Jesus.
- Jesus obeys as Messiah.
- God works through Jesus.
- Jesus dies faithfully.
- God raises and exalts Jesus.
- Jesus mediates between God and humanity.
- Believers come to the Father through the Son.

Resulting argument:

The gospel yields "God through His Messiah." The saving story is not less powerful because Jesus is God's man rather than the God-man. It becomes clearer: the one God has acted decisively through His faithful human Son.

## Final Yield

When the evidence is read cumulatively, it yields this conclusion:

Jesus is not merely a prophet, teacher, or moral example. He is God's supreme agent, Messiah, Son, Lord, Wisdom-bearer, image, mediator, last Adam, and appointed judge. Yet the one God whom Jesus reveals, obeys, worships, prays to, and serves remains the Father.

The New Testament's dominant pattern yields a high but representative Christology: God speaks, acts, saves, judges, reconciles, and reigns through Jesus. The Son yields perfectly to the Father, and all honor given to the Son finally yields glory to the one God who sent him.
