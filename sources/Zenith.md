# Zenith: The Peak Argument of the Project

The highest point of this project is a cumulative claim: the New Testament's exalted language about Jesus is best read within Jewish monotheism, not later Nicene metaphysics. Jesus is God's uniquely begotten, Spirit-anointed, obedient, crucified, risen, and exalted human Messiah. He is God's supreme agent, the embodied Wisdom and Word of God, the image of the invisible God, the appointed judge, the mediator of salvation, and the Lord of the new creation. Yet the one God remains the Father, the God whom Jesus worships, obeys, reveals, and serves.

This conclusion does not diminish Jesus. It relocates his greatness where the apostolic witness places it: not in ontological identity with the one God, but in divine appointment, perfect obedience, resurrection vindication, representative authority, and exalted lordship to the glory of God the Father.

## 1. The Project's Central Question

The whole project turns on one question:

Is Jesus the God-man of later church tradition, eternally one in essence with the Father, or is he God's man, the uniquely authorized Messiah through whom the one God acts?

The project answers by returning to the world of Jesus and the apostles: Israel's Scriptures, Second Temple Jewish monotheism, royal Messiahship, Wisdom literature, agency, resurrection proclamation, and the earliest Christian confession. In that world, divine authority can be mediated without multiplying God's identity. The sent one can speak for the sender without becoming the sender. God's Wisdom can be embodied without requiring a second eternal person within God.

The resulting thesis is simple:

There is one God, the Father; one Lord Messiah, Jesus; and one saving mission accomplished by God through the man whom He sent, raised, and exalted.

## 2. The One God Is the Father

The project's foundation is Father-centered monotheism.

The biblical confession begins with the Shema: "YHWH our God, YHWH is one." Jesus affirms this confession rather than revising it into a tri-personal definition of God. The New Testament repeatedly identifies the one God as the Father while distinguishing Jesus as the one sent by Him.

Core texts:

- Deuteronomy 6:4 and Mark 12:29 preserve Israel's confession of one God.
- John 17:3 has Jesus call the Father "the only true God."
- 1 Corinthians 8:6 distinguishes "one God, the Father" from "one Lord, Jesus Christ."
- 1 Timothy 2:5 distinguishes the one God from the one mediator, "the man Christ Jesus."
- Ephesians 4:6 speaks of "one God and Father of all."

The project therefore begins where Jesus begins: with the Father as the only true God. Every claim about Jesus must be interpreted within that confession, not over it.

## 3. Jesus Is God's Human Messiah

The apostolic proclamation presents Jesus as the man appointed, attested, raised, and exalted by God.

Peter's Pentecost sermon does not announce that God became a man. It announces that Jesus was "a man attested" by God through works God did through him, and that God made this crucified Jesus "both Lord and Christ." Paul likewise proclaims a Davidic Messiah designated Son of God in power by resurrection, and a man through whom God will judge the world.

Core texts:

- Acts 2:22 presents Jesus as a man attested by God.
- Acts 2:36 says God made Jesus Lord and Christ.
- Acts 5:30-31 says God raised and exalted Jesus.
- Acts 10:38 says God anointed Jesus with Holy Spirit and power.
- Acts 17:31 says God will judge the world through a man He appointed.
- Romans 1:3-4 presents Jesus as Davidic Messiah vindicated by resurrection.

The peak argument here is not that Jesus is ordinary. It is that his extraordinary status is God-given. His lordship is real, universal, and saving, but it is derived from the Father and exercised under the Father.

## 4. Agency Explains Divine Representation

The Jewish law of agency, often summarized as "the agent is as the one who sent him," supplies one of the project's strongest interpretive keys.

In Scripture, God's representatives can bear God's name, speak God's words, enact God's judgment, and receive honor connected with God's authority. Moses can be made "God" to Pharaoh. Judges can be called "gods" because they administer divine justice. The angel of YHWH can speak in God's name while remaining God's messenger. None of this requires the agent to be ontologically identical with God.

Jesus is the supreme fulfillment of this pattern.

He is repeatedly described as sent by the Father. He does not speak from himself, act from himself, or glorify himself. He reveals God because God is with him, in him, and acting through him. To receive Jesus is to receive the one who sent him. To honor Jesus is to honor the Father who authorized him.

Agency explains how Jesus can:

- speak God's words;
- reveal God's character;
- forgive sins as God's authorized representative;
- judge on God's behalf;
- receive honor as the Son;
- bear divine titles functionally;
- act as the visible image of the invisible God.

The agency framework preserves both sides of the biblical pattern: Jesus truly represents God, and Jesus remains distinct from God.

## 5. Wisdom and Word Become Embodied in Jesus

The project also argues that Wisdom and Logos language is better understood through Jewish personification and embodiment than through later metaphysical personhood.

Jewish Wisdom literature speaks of God's Wisdom as present with God, active in creation, dwelling among people, and calling humanity to life. This language is poetic, theological, and personified. It does not require Wisdom to be a second divine person alongside God.

John's Logos language can be read within that same world. The Word is God's creative speech, plan, reason, wisdom, and self-expression. The Word "became flesh" when God's eternal purpose came to historical expression in the man Jesus.

Core claims:

- Proverbs 8 personifies Wisdom without making Wisdom a second God.
- Sirach 24 and Wisdom of Solomon 7-10 provide the Jewish background for elevated Wisdom language.
- John 1 can be read as God's Word/Wisdom becoming embodied in Jesus.
- 1 Corinthians 1:24 calls Christ the wisdom of God.
- Colossians 1 and Hebrews 1 use image, firstborn, radiance, and representative language.

The peak argument is that Jesus is not less than the embodiment of God's Wisdom. He is exactly that: God's wise redemptive purpose made visible in a faithful human life.

## 6. High Christology Need Not Mean Ontological Deity

The project does not deny high Christology. It contests what high Christology means.

Jesus is Lord, Son of God, image of God, Wisdom of God, mediator, judge, Savior, firstborn, heir, and exalted ruler. These are immense claims. But in the biblical pattern, high status does not automatically equal identity with the one God.

The key distinction is between functional supremacy and ontological identity.

Functional supremacy means Jesus acts with God's authority, reveals God's will, governs God's new creation, and receives honor because God has exalted him. Ontological identity means Jesus is the same God as the Father in being or essence. The project argues that the first category is deeply biblical, while the second is a later theological synthesis.

This distinction clarifies disputed texts:

- John 1:1 can affirm the divine quality of God's Word without identifying Jesus as a second co-equal person.
- John 8:58 can be read as messianic self-identification and priority in God's plan.
- John 10:30 can mean unity of purpose and mission, especially in light of John 17.
- John 20:28 can function as recognition of the risen Messiah as God's supreme representative.
- Philippians 2:6-11 can portray the obedient Messiah who refuses grasping, humbles himself, and is exalted by God.
- Colossians 1:15-20 can speak of Christ as image, firstborn, heir, head, and agent of new creation.
- Hebrews 1 can exalt the Son as God's final representative without collapsing him into the God he represents.

The project therefore rejects the assumption that every exalted statement about Jesus must be translated into later ontological categories.

## 7. The Gospel Is Exaltation, Not Incarnation Metaphysics

The apostolic gospel centers on what God did through Jesus and for Jesus.

God sent Jesus. God anointed Jesus. God worked through Jesus. God raised Jesus. God exalted Jesus. God made Jesus Lord and Christ. God appointed Jesus as judge. God reconciles the world through Jesus. God is glorified through Jesus.

This is not a lesser gospel. It is the gospel pattern of Acts, Paul, Hebrews, and the Gospels.

The project's gospel summary is:

The one God of Israel fulfilled His promise by sending Jesus, His Son and Messiah. Jesus obeyed God perfectly, proclaimed the Kingdom, died for sins, was raised from the dead, and was exalted to God's right hand as Lord. Through this man, God grants forgiveness, life, judgment, and new creation.

This preserves the human obedience of Jesus as real obedience, his temptation as real testing, his death as real death, and his resurrection as God's decisive vindication.

## 8. The Historical Development Matters

The project also treats Trinitarian doctrine as a historical development rather than the transparent grammar of the New Testament.

The later creeds used technical categories such as essence, substance, hypostasis, eternal generation, and two natures. These terms arose in post-apostolic debates, especially as the church engaged Greek philosophical categories and internal disputes over Christ's status.

This does not mean later theologians were insincere. It means their framework should not be imposed backward as the default meaning of the biblical text.

The project distinguishes:

- apostolic confession: one God, the Father, and one Lord Messiah, Jesus;
- Jewish interpretive categories: agency, Wisdom, Messiahship, exaltation, representation;
- later doctrinal categories: Trinity, consubstantiality, eternal generation, hypostatic union.

The peak historical claim is that Biblical Unitarianism better preserves the grammar of the first-century confession, while Trinitarianism reflects a later synthesis of biblical language with post-biblical metaphysics.

## 9. The Cumulative Case

No single argument carries the whole project alone. The strength is cumulative.

The case rises through repeated patterns:

- The one God is identified as the Father.
- Jesus is distinguished from God as Son, servant, mediator, Messiah, and man.
- Jesus depends on God, prays to God, obeys God, is sent by God, and is raised by God.
- Divine agency allows God's representative to bear God's authority.
- Wisdom and Word language allows God's plan to be embodied without requiring a second divine person.
- Resurrection and exaltation explain Jesus' supreme lordship.
- The disputed proof texts can be read coherently without redefining God as triune.
- Later Trinitarian doctrine uses categories not explicit in the apostolic proclamation.

Together these points form the project's zenith:

The New Testament can affirm the highest possible role for Jesus under God without making Jesus the one God Himself.

## Final Thesis

The project reaches its peak in this confession:

There is one God, the Father, the only true God. Jesus is His Son, His Messiah, His agent, His image, His Wisdom embodied, His appointed judge, His exalted Lord, and the mediator through whom salvation comes. Jesus reveals God perfectly because God acts through him perfectly. Jesus is worthy of honor because the Father has given him authority. Jesus is central to God's redemptive plan because in him God's Word, Wisdom, Kingdom, and new creation come to their appointed fulfillment.

Therefore, the best reading of the biblical evidence is not that Jesus is God Himself, but that Jesus is God's supreme human representative: the one whom God sent, empowered, raised, exalted, and appointed over all things.

This is the project's zenith: the highest Christology that remains faithful to the one God of Israel is not "God the Son," but the Son of God.
