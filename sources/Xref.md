# Cross-References for Core Supporting Arguments

This file gathers the strongest cross-references supporting the project's main thesis: Jesus is best read within Jewish monotheism as God's uniquely authorized human Messiah, supreme agent, and embodiment of God's Wisdom/Word, rather than as the later Nicene "God-man" in an ontological sense.

The references below are selective rather than exhaustive. They prioritize texts that carry the most argumentative weight across the project files.

## 1. The One God Is the Father

Core claim: The New Testament repeatedly identifies the one God as the Father, while distinguishing Jesus as the one sent, authorized, raised, and exalted by God.

Strong biblical cross-references:

- Deuteronomy 6:4 - Israel's foundational confession: YHWH is one.
- Mark 12:29-34 - Jesus affirms the Shema without redefining God as tri-personal.
- John 17:3 - Jesus calls the Father "the only true God" and distinguishes himself as the one sent by Him.
- 1 Corinthians 8:6 - "One God, the Father" and "one Lord, Jesus Christ" distinguish source and agent.
- 1 Timothy 2:5 - "One God" and "one mediator... the man Christ Jesus."
- Ephesians 4:6 - "One God and Father of all."

Extrabiblical and historical cross-references:

- Second Temple Jewish monotheism - The wider Jewish matrix assumes one sovereign God without ontological partners.
- James F. McGrath, *The Only True God* - Useful for early Christian monotheism in Jewish context.
- Dale Tuggy, "Trinity," *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy* - Defines later Trinitarian claims and highlights their post-biblical conceptual grammar.

## 2. Jesus Is God's Human Messiah

Core claim: The apostolic proclamation presents Jesus as the man approved, empowered, raised, and appointed by God.

Strong biblical cross-references:

- Acts 2:22 - Jesus is "a man attested" by God through works God did through him.
- Acts 2:36 - God made Jesus both Lord and Christ.
- Acts 3:13, 26 - Jesus is God's servant.
- Acts 17:31 - God will judge the world through a man He appointed.
- Romans 1:3-4 - Jesus is Davidic Messiah, designated Son of God in power by resurrection.
- Hebrews 2:14-18; 4:15 - Jesus shares genuine humanity, suffering, and testing.

Extrabiblical and historical cross-references:

- Davidic royal ideology - 2 Samuel 7 and Psalm 2 background the Messiah as God's appointed son and king.
- Apostolic Fathers - Frequently distinguish God the Father from Jesus the Messiah in subordinationist patterns.

## 3. Shaliah Agency Explains Divine Representation

Core claim: In Jewish agency, the authorized representative can speak and act for the sender without becoming the sender ontologically.

Strong biblical cross-references:

- Exodus 3:2-6 - The angel of YHWH appears, yet the encounter is narrated as God's speech.
- Exodus 7:1 - Moses is made "God" to Pharaoh representationally.
- Exodus 23:20-21 - God's angel bears God's name.
- Psalm 82:1, 6 - Human rulers or judges can be called "gods" because they represent divine justice.
- John 5:19-23 - The Son acts because the Father grants him authority.
- John 7:16-18 - Jesus' teaching is from the one who sent him.
- John 10:34-36 - Jesus uses Psalm 82 and "sent" language to defend Sonship.
- John 13:16, 20 - Receiving the sent one means receiving the sender.
- Hebrews 3:1-2 - Jesus is the faithful apostle, the sent representative.

Extrabiblical and historical cross-references:

- Kiddushin 41b - Rabbinic agency dictum: an agent acts on behalf of the sender.
- Nedarim 72b - Another rabbinic witness to agency logic.
- Jewish Encyclopedia, "Agency, Law of" - Summarizes the legal principle that a person's agent is like himself.
- Raymond Essoe, *Shaliah* - Used in the project as a focused source on agency.

## 4. Wisdom and Word Language Need Not Mean a Second Divine Person

Core claim: Jewish Wisdom and Word traditions can describe God's creative, revelatory, and redemptive purpose in vivid personal language without requiring another divine person.

Strong biblical cross-references:

- Proverbs 8:22-31 - Wisdom is personified as present with God in creation.
- Job 28 - Wisdom is hidden with God and belongs to Him.
- John 1:1-18 - The Word becomes flesh in Jesus; the Unitarian reading treats Logos as God's self-expression embodied historically.
- 1 Corinthians 1:24, 30 - Christ is God's wisdom and becomes wisdom from God for believers.
- Colossians 1:15 - Jesus is the image of the invisible God.
- Hebrews 1:1-3 - God speaks in a Son who is radiance and representation.

Extrabiblical and Second Temple cross-references:

- Sirach 24 - Wisdom proceeds from God and dwells among God's people.
- Baruch 3:29-4:4 - Wisdom is associated with God's instruction and Israel's covenant life.
- Wisdom of Solomon 7:22-8:1 - Wisdom is described as radiance, reflection, and image of God's goodness.
- Philo's Logos language - A Jewish-Hellenistic example of elevated intermediary language that does not simply equal Nicene ontology.
- James D. G. Dunn, *Christology in the Making* - Important for Logos/Wisdom Christology not necessarily beginning as personal preexistence.

## 5. Preexistence Can Be Ideal, Prophetic, or Foreordained

Core claim: Some Jewish and biblical texts can speak of people or realities as existing beforehand in God's plan, promise, Wisdom, or decree before appearing historically.

Strong biblical cross-references:

- Jeremiah 1:5 - Jeremiah is known before birth without implying literal personal preexistence.
- Romans 4:17 - God calls things that do not exist as though they exist.
- Ephesians 1:4 - Believers are chosen before the foundation of the world.
- 1 Peter 1:20 - Christ is foreknown before the foundation of the world and manifested in the last times.
- Revelation 13:8 - Lamb imagery tied to God's pre-temporal redemptive purpose.
- John 17:5, 24 - Glory language can be read through divine foreordination and promised destiny.

Extrabiblical and Second Temple cross-references:

- Jewish apocalyptic and wisdom traditions - Often speak of realities reserved, prepared, or foreknown with God.
- Rabbinic traditions about things created or planned before the world - Useful conceptual background for ideal preexistence.

## 6. John 1 and the Logos

Core claim: John 1 can be read as God's Word, Wisdom, plan, or self-expression becoming embodied in Jesus, not necessarily as an eternal second person becoming human.

Strong biblical cross-references:

- Genesis 1 - God creates by speaking.
- Psalm 33:6 - The heavens are made by the word of YHWH.
- Isaiah 55:10-11 - God's word goes forth and accomplishes His purpose.
- Proverbs 8 - Wisdom language behind Logos interpretation.
- John 1:14 - The Word becomes flesh in the historical Jesus.
- John 1:18 - The Son reveals the unseen God.

Extrabiblical and Second Temple cross-references:

- Sirach 24; Wisdom of Solomon 7-10 - Wisdom as God's self-expression and creative order.
- Targumic Memra traditions - God's "Word" functions as a reverent way to speak of divine action.
- Philo - Logos as an intermediary concept in Jewish-Hellenistic thought.

## 9. John 8:58 and "I Am"

Core claim: The Greek phrase *ego eimi* can function as self-identification or messianic-revelatory declaration without necessarily being a direct claim to the divine name.

Strong biblical cross-references:

- John 8:58 - "Before Abraham was, I am."
- John 9:9 - The healed blind man uses *ego eimi* for self-identification.
- John 4:25-26 - Jesus identifies himself as Messiah with similar "I am" language.
- John 18:5-8 - The phrase functions dramatically in the arrest scene.
- Exodus 3:14 - The divine-name background often appealed to by Trinitarian readings.

Extrabiblical and scholarly cross-references:

- C. K. Barrett on *ego eimi* - The project cites Barrett for the view that the phrase draws attention to Jesus without simply identifying him as God.
- Greek idiom usage - *ego eimi* can mean "I am he" or "It is I" depending on context.

## 10. Philippians 2 and Obedient Messiah/Second Adam

Core claim: Philippians 2 can be read not as a descent from divine preexistence but as the pattern of the obedient human Messiah who refuses grasping and is therefore exalted by God.

Strong biblical cross-references:

- Genesis 3:5-6 - Adam grasps at being like God.
- Isaiah 52:13-53:12 - The suffering servant is humbled and then exalted.
- Philippians 2:5-11 - Jesus humbles himself; God highly exalts him.
- Romans 5:12-19 - Adam and Christ contrast.
- 1 Corinthians 15:45-49 - Christ as last Adam.
- Philippians 2:11 - Jesus' lordship is confessed "to the glory of God the Father."

Extrabiblical and scholarly cross-references:

- Adam Christology - A recognized interpretive framework for reading the hymn through human obedience rather than divine descent.
- Discussions of *morphe* - Lexical debates over "form" as appearance/status versus ontology.

## 14. Jesus' Genuine Humanity and Mediation

Core claim: Biblical Unitarianism gives direct force to Jesus' growth, dependence, testing, obedience, death, and mediation as truly human realities.

Strong biblical cross-references:

- Luke 2:40, 52 - Jesus grows in wisdom and stature.
- Mark 13:32 - The Son does not know the day or hour.
- John 14:28 - The Father is greater than Jesus.
- Matthew 26:39 - Jesus submits his will to the Father.
- Hebrews 5:7-9 - Jesus learns obedience through suffering.
- 1 Timothy 2:5 - The mediator is "the man Christ Jesus."
- 1 Corinthians 15:21-22, 45-49 - Resurrection comes through a man, the last Adam.

Extrabiblical and historical cross-references:

- Two-natures Christology - Later conciliar theology explains humanity and deity through metaphysical categories not stated directly in the New Testament.
- Chalcedon 451 - Later formal statement of one person in two natures.

## 16. Divine Titles Can Be Representative Rather Than Ontological

Core claim: Titles such as God, god, Lord, Son of God, image of God, and firstborn must be interpreted by context and biblical usage, not assumed to carry Nicene metaphysics.

Strong biblical cross-references:

- Exodus 7:1 - Moses as "God" to Pharaoh.
- Psalm 45:6-7 - Royal "God" language applied to an anointed king who has a God.
- Psalm 82:6 - "You are gods" applied to representatives of divine judgment.
- Psalm 110:1 - Distinction between YHWH and the Messiah addressed as lord.
- John 10:34-36 - Jesus' own use of Psalm 82.
- 2 Corinthians 4:4; Colossians 1:15 - Image language as representation.

Extrabiblical and historical cross-references:

- Jewish agency law - Divine titles may be carried representationally by authorized agents.
- Royal ideology in the ancient Near East - Kings can bear divine-like titles without being identical to the high God.

## 17. The Trinity Uses Later Technical Categories

Core claim: Terms required for classical Trinitarian definition are later doctrinal tools, not the natural vocabulary of the New Testament.

Strong biblical cross-references:

- Acts 2:22-36; 10:38-42; 17:31 - Apostolic preaching defines Jesus by God's action through, for, and upon him.
- Matthew 28:19; 2 Corinthians 13:14 - Triadic formulas mention Father, Son, and Spirit but do not define one God as three co-equal persons.
- 1 Corinthians 15:24-28 - Final subordination of the Son to God.

Extrabiblical and historical cross-references:

- Council of Nicaea 325 - Formal use of *homoousios* for the Son's relation to the Father.
- Council of Constantinople 381 - Consolidation of pro-Nicene Trinitarian confession including the Spirit.
- Chalcedon 451 - Two-natures Christology.
- New Catholic Encyclopedia - Cited in the project for the late assimilation of the "one God in three Persons" formulation.
- Encyclopedia Americana - Cited in the project for the claim that fourth-century Trinitarianism was a deviation from earlier Jewish-Christian unitarianism.
- New Schaff-Herzog Encyclopedia - Cited in the project for Greek philosophical influence on Logos and Trinity doctrine.
- Britannica, "First Council of Nicaea" - Historical overview of Nicaea and *homoousios*.

Strong biblical cross-references:

- Acts 2:17-18 - God pours out His Spirit.
- Acts 10:38 - God anoints Jesus with Holy Spirit and power.
- Romans 8:9-11 - Spirit of God active in believers.

Extrabiblical and historical cross-references:

- Constantinople 381 - Later formalization of the Spirit's place in Trinitarian doctrine.
- Hebrew *ruach* background - Spirit as breath, wind, power, and divine presence.

## 19. The Gospel of the Kingdom Centers on God Acting Through His Messiah

Core claim: The apostolic gospel is not first a metaphysical doctrine of divine essence; it is the announcement that the God of Israel has acted through Jesus, raised him, and appointed him Lord and judge.

Strong biblical cross-references:

- Matthew 16:16 - Jesus is confessed as the Christ, the Son of the living God.
- Luke 4:43 - Jesus is sent to preach the Kingdom of God.
- Luke 24:46-49 - Repentance and forgiveness are proclaimed in the Messiah's name.
- Acts 1:3 - The risen Jesus teaches about the Kingdom of God.
- Acts 2:22-36 - God raises and enthrones Jesus.
- Romans 10:9 - Confess Jesus as Lord and believe God raised him.
- 1 Corinthians 15:3-8, 20-28 - Death, resurrection, reign, and final subjection to God.

Extrabiblical and historical cross-references:

- Jewish messianic expectation - Kingdom, Davidic sonship, judgment, and restoration frame Jesus' mission.
- Early Christian kerygma - Apostolic proclamation emphasizes resurrection and lordship rather than later conciliar metaphysics.

## 20. Cumulative Case

Core claim: No single supporting argument bears the entire weight. The project's strongest case is cumulative: Father-centered monotheism, Jesus' humanity, delegated authority, Wisdom embodiment, exaltation, and later doctrinal development mutually reinforce one another.

Strong biblical cross-reference cluster:

- Deuteronomy 6:4; Mark 12:29 - One God.
- John 17:3; 1 Corinthians 8:6; 1 Timothy 2:5 - Father as one God, Jesus as Lord/mediator.
- Acts 2:22-36; 10:38-42; 17:31 - God acts through, raises, and appoints Jesus.
- John 5; 7; 10; 13; 17 - Sent-agent Christology.
- Proverbs 8; Sirach 24; Wisdom of Solomon 7-10; John 1; Colossians 1; Hebrews 1 - Wisdom/Word/image language.
- Philippians 2; 1 Corinthians 15 - Obedience, exaltation, and final subordination.

Strong extrabiblical and historical cluster:

- Kiddushin 41b; Nedarim 72b; Jewish Encyclopedia, "Agency, Law of" - Shaliah agency.
- Sirach 24; Wisdom of Solomon 7-10; Baruch 3-4; Philo - Wisdom/Logos and intermediary language.
- Dunn, McGrath, Hurtado, Bauckham - Major modern discussion partners on early Christology, monotheism, devotion, and divine identity.
- Nicaea 325; Constantinople 381; Chalcedon 451 - Later conciliar development.
