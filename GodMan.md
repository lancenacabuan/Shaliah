# God-Man or God's Man: Trinitarian and Biblical Unitarian Christologies in Light of Second Temple Wisdom and Agency

## 1. Framing the Question and the Historical-Literary Method

This section introduces the central question: whether Jesus should be understood as the **“God-man”** of later Trinitarian doctrine or as **“God’s man,”** God’s uniquely authorized human Messiah. It argues that later doctrinal categories such as “substance,” “hypostasis,” and “two natures” should not control the reading of the New Testament. Instead, the study proposes reading Jesus through categories already available in Second Temple Judaism: **Wisdom/Word language** and **divine agency**.

## 2. Second Temple Categories: Wisdom, Word, and Intermediary Language

This chapter explains how Jewish texts could speak of God’s action in the world through personified concepts like **Wisdom**, **Word/Logos**, and divine presence without abandoning monotheism. Wisdom literature often describes Wisdom in highly personal language, but the author argues this is poetic and theological language for God’s own self-expression, not proof of another divine person. This provides a framework for reading New Testament claims about the Word or Wisdom becoming embodied in Jesus.

## 3. Agency and Representation: Shaliaḥ and Sent Christology

This section develops the idea of **agency**, especially the Jewish concept that a messenger represents the sender. Jesus is presented as God’s authorized agent: sent by God, speaking God’s words, doing God’s works, and receiving authority from God. The author argues that this explains why Jesus can bear divine authority and even receive exalted language without being God ontologically. Texts like John 17:3, 1 Corinthians 8:6, 1 Timothy 2:5, and Acts 2:22 are used to support this sender-agent distinction.

## 4. High Christology Revisited Through Wisdom and Agency

This chapter addresses major “high Christology” passages often used to support Trinitarian doctrine. John 1, Hebrews 1, Philippians 2, Colossians 1, John 20:28, Hebrews 1:8, Titus 2:13, and 2 Peter 1:1 are interpreted through Wisdom and agency rather than later ontological categories. The author argues these texts can affirm Jesus’ exalted role, divine mission, and unique authority without requiring that Jesus is God by nature.

## 5. From Apostolic Proclamation to Conciliar Orthodoxy

This section argues that formal Trinitarian doctrine developed later through fourth- and fifth-century theological controversies. Terms like **homoousios**, **hypostasis**, and “two natures” are described as post-apostolic tools, not New Testament language. The author’s point is not that later doctrine is meaningless, but that it should not be treated as a neutral reading lens for first-century Jewish texts.

## 6. Which Reading Is More Sensible?

The final chapter concludes that the Biblical Unitarian reading is more historically and literarily natural. It argues that the New Testament repeatedly distinguishes **the Father as the one God** from **Jesus as the sent Messiah and mediator**. Jesus is not diminished in this model; rather, he is understood as the perfectly obedient human Messiah who embodies God’s wisdom, reveals God’s character, is raised and exalted by God, and serves as God’s appointed Lord and mediator.

## Overall Thesis

The document argues that Jesus is best understood not as the later Trinitarian **God-man**, but as **God’s man**: the human Messiah who uniquely embodies God’s Wisdom, represents God as His supreme agent, and receives exalted lordship through resurrection and divine appointment.
